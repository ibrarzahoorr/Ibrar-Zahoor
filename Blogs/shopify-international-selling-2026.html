Selling Internationally on Shopify: A 2026 Setup Guide That Avoids the Common Mistakes

Expanding a Shopify store into new countries used to require a separate store per region and a lot of manual workarounds. Shopify Markets changed that, but "turning it on" and doing it properly are two different things. Here's how to set up multi-currency, local payments, duties, translation, and shipping the right way — and the mistakes that quietly cost stores their international conversion rate.

Shopify Markets: The Foundation

Shopify Markets lets you manage multiple countries or regions from a single store and admin, rather than running separate stores per market. It's the backbone for everything else in this guide — currency, payment methods, duties, domains, and content variations are all configured per-market inside one Shopify instance.

Setup starts with grouping countries into markets that make sense for your business (e.g., "North America," "EU," "UK," "Australia/NZ" as separate markets rather than one giant "rest of world" bucket). Each market gets its own currency, domain/subfolder structure if desired, and can have its own pricing, product availability, and shipping rates. The mistake I see most is treating Markets as a single on/off switch instead of actually configuring each region's specifics — currency alone doesn't make a store feel local.

Multi-Currency & Local Payment Methods

Displaying prices in a shopper's local currency is one of the highest-leverage changes for international conversion — customers consistently convert better when they see a final price in their own currency rather than doing mental math from USD. Shopify Payments handles the currency conversion and settlement automatically once markets are configured with local currencies.

Payment methods matter just as much as currency. A checkout that only offers credit cards will underperform in markets where local payment methods dominate:

  • Europe: iDEAL (Netherlands), Klarna and SOFORT (Germany/Nordics), Bancontact (Belgium)
  • UK: strong preference for cards plus PayPal and buy-now-pay-later options
  • Southeast Asia: GrabPay, local e-wallets, cash-on-delivery in several markets
  • Middle East: cash-on-delivery remains significant, alongside card and local wallets

Shop Pay, Apple Pay, and Google Pay reduce checkout friction broadly, but the region-specific methods above are often the difference between a shopper completing checkout or abandoning at the payment step entirely. Configure the relevant methods per market rather than offering one global checkout for every region — see our multi-currency setup guide for the specific configuration steps.

Duties, Taxes & Landed Cost

Nothing kills international conversion faster than a customer discovering an unexpected customs bill after their package leaves the country. Shopify Markets Pro (and various duty-calculation apps for stores not on Markets Pro) can calculate and collect duties and import taxes at checkout, so the price the customer pays is the full landed cost — no surprises at the door.

Rule of thumb: for any market where you ship meaningful volume, show duties/taxes at checkout rather than leaving them to be collected on delivery. The upfront number is always less painful than an unexpected one after the sale.

Beyond duties, VAT/GST registration thresholds vary by country and change periodically — the EU's VAT rules for cross-border sales, UK VAT post-Brexit, and Australia's GST on low-value imports are all handled differently. This is genuinely one of the areas worth a short consultation with an accountant familiar with cross-border e-commerce tax before scaling into a new region; getting it wrong retroactively is far more expensive than setting it up correctly from day one.

It's also worth deciding early whether a given market needs a local tax registration or legal entity at all, versus selling cross-border under your existing entity. Many stores start cross-border (no local registration, duties collected at checkout or on delivery) and only set up a local entity once volume in that market justifies the added accounting overhead — jumping straight to full local incorporation before you know a market will work is a common way to overspend on compliance for a market that never takes off.

Translating Your Storefront

1

Start with high-impact pages

Homepage, top collection pages, and your best-selling product pages first — not a blanket translation of every page on day one. Prioritize by where international traffic actually lands.

2

Use Shopify's Translate & Adapt (or a paid app) for scale

Machine translation gets you 80% of the way for most product descriptions; native review of key pages (homepage copy, policies, top sellers) catches the tone and idiom machine translation misses.

3

Localize, don't just translate

Sizing charts, date formats, measurement units, and even color/imagery associations vary by culture. A literal translation without localization often reads as generic or slightly "off" to native speakers.

4

Translate transactional content too

Order confirmation emails, shipping notifications, and return policy pages get real traffic post-purchase — leaving them in English while the storefront is localized is a common gap.

Region-Specific Shipping Strategy

Shipping is where a lot of international expansion plans quietly fail. A few practical guidelines:

  • Set honest delivery estimates per region — a generic "5-7 business days" applied globally will be wrong for most destinations and creates support tickets and chargebacks when it's wrong.
  • Use regional fulfillment where volume justifies it — a local 3PL or fulfillment center in a high-volume market cuts delivery time and cost dramatically compared to shipping every order from a single origin country.
  • Price shipping to match market expectations — free-shipping thresholds that work domestically may not make sense once international carrier costs are added; consider per-market free-shipping thresholds rather than one global number.
  • Communicate proactively about longer transit times — international customers are generally more tolerant of longer delivery windows when the estimate is accurate and tracked, but not when the number turns out to be fiction.

Customer Support Across Time Zones and Languages

International expansion quietly multiplies your support load in ways that are easy to underestimate before launch. A customer in Sydney emailing at what's 3am for your domestic team either waits half a day for a reply or gets no response until the next business day — and international shoppers, who often already feel less certain about ordering from a brand outside their country, are quicker to abandon or chargeback when support feels absent.

  • Set honest response-time expectations per region in your help center rather than a single generic "24-48 hours" that's wrong for half your markets.
  • Use a shared helpdesk with tagging by market so patterns specific to one region (a courier issue, a customs delay) get spotted quickly rather than treated as isolated tickets.
  • Prioritize language support for your top 2-3 non-English markets first — even basic templated responses in the local language reduce friction more than people expect, well before you can justify native-language full-time support.
  • Make your returns policy region-specific and visible before checkout, not just at the point a customer wants to return something — ambiguity here is one of the biggest pre-purchase objections for international buyers.

Common Global-Expansion Mistakes

  • Turning on every country at once. Launching 20 markets simultaneously means shallow execution everywhere. Pick 2-3 priority markets, get currency/payments/shipping/duties genuinely right, then expand.
  • Ignoring local competitors and price expectations. A price that's competitive domestically may be high or low relative to the local market once currency and shipping are factored in.
  • Underestimating returns logistics. International returns are expensive and slow if not planned for — decide on your return policy per region before launch, not after the first return request arrives.
  • Skipping local customer support hours/language. Even basic email support in the local language and reasonable response times during that region's business hours meaningfully affects trust and conversion.
  • Assuming one product-market fit translates everywhere. A product that sells well domestically can perform very differently abroad once local competitors, price sensitivity, and cultural fit are factored in — validate demand with a smaller ad spend or pre-launch waitlist before committing full inventory and marketing budget to a new market.

FAQ

Do I need Shopify Plus to sell internationally?

No. Shopify Markets is available on standard plans; Shopify Markets Pro (with built-in duty calculation and expanded compliance tools) has broader availability requirements, but core international selling works on any plan.

Should I use subdomains, subfolders, or separate ccTLD domains for each market?

Subfolders (yourstore.com/en-gb/) are usually the simplest to manage and preserve domain authority. Separate ccTLDs (yourstore.co.uk) can help with local trust signals but multiply the SEO and maintenance workload.

How many markets should a store launch with initially?

Two to three well-executed markets beat ten shallow ones. Expand once currency, payments, shipping, and support are genuinely dialed in for your first markets.
