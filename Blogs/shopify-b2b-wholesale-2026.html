Company Accounts: The Core of Shopify B2B

Company accounts let a single business customer have multiple individual buyers (a purchasing manager, a store location, an assistant) all shopping under one company profile, with shared payment terms, price lists, and order history. This is the feature that finally let Shopify compete with dedicated B2B platforms — before it existed, most wholesale Shopify stores hacked together customer tags and app-based workarounds that never handled multi-location or multi-buyer accounts cleanly.

  • Locations. A company can have multiple locations (e.g. different retail branches), each with its own shipping address, tax status, and even assigned price list — critical for chains and franchises ordering from one wholesale account.
  • Roles and permissions. You can control which buyers at a company can place orders, view pricing, or only browse — useful for larger accounts where a purchasing team has different authority levels.
  • Order history per company. Buyers and account managers can see the full company-wide order history in one place, which matters a lot for repeat wholesale ordering and reorder-from-history workflows.
  • Self-serve reordering. Because buyers can see prior orders and assigned pricing directly, many companies place repeat orders entirely without a sales rep involved — freeing your team to focus on new account acquisition and larger negotiated deals instead of routine reorder processing.

The practical effect of this shift is that a wholesale relationship no longer requires a phone call or a manually typed purchase order for every reorder. A returning buyer can log into their company account, see their negotiated pricing automatically applied, reorder their usual case quantities in a couple of clicks, and check out on agreed payment terms — the entire cycle that used to require an account manager's involvement at every step.

Custom Price Lists and Quantity Rules

1

Create a price list

Build a catalog-specific price list — a flat percentage discount off retail, or fully custom fixed pricing per SKU — and assign it to specific companies or locations rather than applying it storewide.

2

Set quantity rules

Define minimum order quantities, maximum limits, and quantity increments (e.g. "must order in multiples of 12") per product or variant — standard for wholesale, where case-pack sizes matter.

3

Layer in volume pricing

Set tiered discounts that automatically apply as order quantity increases (e.g. 5% off at 50 units, 10% off at 200) — encouraging larger orders without manual quote negotiation for every deal.

4

Restrict catalog visibility

Hide certain products or entire collections from specific companies, so a regional distributor only sees the products relevant to their market or contract.

Why this matters: before native quantity rules and price lists, wholesale Shopify stores relied on manual invoicing or third-party apps stacking fees on top of each other. Doing this natively removes an entire layer of app dependency and monthly cost.

Net Payment Terms and B2B Checkout

Shopify B2B supports net payment terms (Net 7, 15, 30, 60, 90) directly at checkout, letting approved company accounts place an order and pay the invoice later rather than paying immediately by card — the standard expectation in most wholesale and distributor relationships. Orders placed on terms generate a proper invoice with a due date, and outstanding balances are tracked per company in the admin.

This single feature is often the deciding factor for brands migrating off a legacy B2B platform, since offering payment terms was previously one of the hardest things to bolt onto a standard Shopify checkout without custom development or a third-party invoicing app. It also removes a real cash-flow risk: because Shopify tracks the due date and outstanding balance automatically, you get visibility into overdue accounts without manually cross-referencing a separate accounting system, which matters a great deal once you have dozens of wholesale accounts on staggered terms.

When You Actually Need Shopify Plus for B2B

Native B2B functionality (company accounts, price lists, quantity rules, net terms) is a Shopify Plus feature — it is not available on standard Shopify plans. If your wholesale volume is still small, a standard plan combined with a wholesale app or customer-tag-based discounting can work as a stopgap, but you'll hit real limits once you need true multi-location company accounts or checkout-level net terms.

Plus becomes worth the higher price point once you have recurring wholesale revenue that justifies removing app fees and manual invoice tracking, or once B2B is a meaningful share of total revenue rather than a handful of accounts. Migrating from a Separate Wholesale Platform or App

A large share of B2B Shopify projects in 2026 are migrations — brands moving off a dedicated wholesale platform, a password-protected duplicate store, or a patchwork of discount-code apps once native company accounts became robust enough to replace them. The migration itself has a few predictable trouble spots worth planning for upfront:

  • Historical order data. Company-level order history doesn't retroactively populate for orders placed before company accounts existed — decide early whether historical wholesale orders need to be imported or if you're starting the company record clean from go-live.
  • Existing customer segmentation. If wholesale customers were previously identified by tags or a separate login system, that logic needs to be rebuilt as company accounts and assigned price lists — this is usually the single largest chunk of migration effort.
  • Sales rep workflows. If your team places orders on behalf of buyers (common in traditional wholesale), confirm your process for draft orders or rep-assisted checkout maps cleanly onto Shopify's company account structure before cutting over.
  • Parallel running period. Keep the old system available in read-only mode for at least one billing cycle after cutover, so outstanding invoices and historical lookups aren't lost mid-transition.
Planning tip: map every existing wholesale customer to their correct price list and location structure in a spreadsheet before touching the Shopify admin — doing this mapping work first prevents the most common go-live delays.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need Shopify Plus to sell wholesale on Shopify?

Not strictly — you can approximate wholesale pricing on standard plans using customer tags and discount apps. But native company accounts, price lists, quantity rules, and net payment terms are Shopify Plus-exclusive features, and they remove most of the complexity of the app-based workarounds once your wholesale volume justifies the upgrade.

Can B2B and regular retail customers use the same Shopify store?

Yes — this is now the standard approach. Company accounts and price lists let approved B2B buyers see wholesale pricing and terms while regular retail customers see the normal storefront, all from one product catalog and one admin, with no risk of retail shoppers accidentally seeing wholesale pricing.

How do quantity rules affect small retail orders on the same store?

Quantity rules and price lists are scoped to specific companies or customer groups, so they don't affect regular retail checkout at all — a retail shopper never sees case-pack minimums or wholesale pricing unless they're logged into an approved company account.

How long does a typical Shopify B2B setup or migration take?

A straightforward setup with a handful of price lists and standard quantity rules can be configured in one to two weeks. A full migration from a legacy wholesale platform, including customer data mapping and rep workflow rebuilding, more commonly takes four to eight weeks depending on catalog size and how many custom pricing arrangements need to be recreated individually.
