Do You Actually Need a Page Builder App?

This is the question worth answering honestly before spending a dollar on any app in this category. Since Online Store 2.0 rolled out, Shopify themes ship with genuinely flexible native sections and blocks — drag-and-drop-editable in the theme customizer, reorderable, and available on every template including custom landing pages, not just the homepage. For a large share of stores, this is already enough: image-with-text blocks, multi-column feature grids, testimonial sliders, FAQ accordions, and countdown banners are all achievable with a modern OS 2.0 theme's native section library plus a small number of well-chosen block-based apps.

Where native sections fall short is genuinely complex, highly custom landing page layouts — asymmetric grids, interactive product configurators, heavily animated storytelling pages for a product launch, or layouts that need pixel-level control a theme's section settings simply don't expose. That's the real use case for a page builder app. If your actual need is "make my homepage look a bit different" or "add a comparison table," that's native-section territory, not page-builder territory — and a surprising number of merchants pay for GemPages or Shogun to build things Dawn's native sections already handle.

The Page Speed Impact Nobody Puts in the Marketing Copy

This is the most common complaint from merchants who've used a page builder for six months or more, and it's a legitimate one. Page builder apps generate their own DOM structure and load their own CSS/JS runtime on top of your theme, and pages built with them are often measurably heavier and slower than the same content built with native sections — sometimes by 100-300ms or more of added load time, occasionally worse depending on how many builder elements are stacked on one page.

Part of this is architectural: builders need a flexible, app-agnostic rendering layer to support their drag-and-drop editor, which is inherently less lean than a theme's purpose-built Liquid templates. Part of it is scope creep — merchants add more embedded widgets, animations, and third-party elements to builder pages over time because the tool makes it easy to keep adding, not because the page needs them. If page speed and Core Web Vitals matter for your SEO or conversion rate (they generally do), audit builder-built pages with PageSpeed Insights specifically, not just your theme's native pages — the numbers are often meaningfully different.

Which App Fits Which Use Case

1

One-Off Landing Pages for Ad Campaigns

PageFly or GemPages are reasonable here — you need fast, flexible layout control for a page that lives outside your normal theme templates, and the monthly cost is justified by ad campaign ROI if the page converts.

2

Non-Developer Teams Needing Ongoing Layout Control

Any of the three works, but GemPages' template quality and PageFly's gentler learning curve suit marketing teams without a developer on staff who need to self-serve new pages regularly.

3

Larger Stores Wanting Built-In A/B Testing

Shogun's testing tools are more mature out of the box, which matters if you're regularly testing landing page variants and don't want to bolt on a separate testing app.

4

Standard Homepage, Collection, or Product Page Layouts

Skip the page builder. A well-built Online Store 2.0 theme with custom sections (built by a developer if you need something specific) will be faster, cheaper long-term, and won't create a dependency where uninstalling the app breaks your page layouts.

The Lock-In Risk Worth Knowing About

Pages built inside a page builder app are usually rendered through that app's own liquid template and script — uninstall the app, and those pages typically break or revert to blank, because the layout data lives in the app's system, not natively in your theme. This is a real switching cost that doesn't show up in any pricing comparison: a store that builds dozens of pages in Shogun over two years faces real rebuild work if it ever decides to migrate away. Weigh this against the convenience before building your entire content strategy inside a third-party builder.

This also affects switching between builders, not just moving away from one entirely. PageFly pages don't migrate cleanly into GemPages and vice versa — each app stores layout data in its own proprietary format, so changing builders mid-project usually means rebuilding pages from scratch rather than exporting and importing. Factor this into the initial choice: picking the right builder up front avoids a costly re-platform later if the first choice turns out to be a poor fit.

Pricing Deep Dive: What You Actually Pay For at Scale

Entry-level pricing is only part of the real cost. Most builders gate meaningful features — number of published pages, A/B testing, removing the app's own branding, priority support — behind higher tiers, and stores frequently find themselves upgrading within a few months of adopting one. A store publishing 15-20 campaign landing pages a year, with A/B testing and no visible app branding, is realistically looking at a mid-to-upper pricing tier on any of these three apps, often $50-$100+/month rather than the advertised entry price.

Compare that ongoing cost against a one-time developer investment in a small library of custom, reusable Online Store 2.0 sections tailored to your actual campaign needs — often cheaper over a 12-18 month horizon, faster-loading, and with no monthly recurring fee or lock-in risk. Neither approach is universally right; the honest comparison has to include the builder's ongoing cost trajectory, not just its cheapest advertised tier.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use Online Store 2.0 sections and a page builder app together?

Yes, most stores do exactly this — native sections for standard pages (home, collections, product), a page builder reserved for specific campaign landing pages that need layout flexibility beyond the theme's section options.

Do page builder apps hurt SEO?

Not inherently, but the added page weight and script overhead can hurt Core Web Vitals scores, which are a minor ranking factor and a real conversion factor. Most builders support basic on-page SEO fields (title, meta description, alt text) fine.

Is it worth switching from a page builder back to native sections?

Often yes, if your pages are mostly standard layouts the builder happens to be handling. The migration takes developer time to rebuild pages as native sections, but it typically pays back through faster load times and one less recurring app fee.

Can a free plan on PageFly or GemPages actually work long-term?

For a very small store with one or two static pages, possibly — but free tiers cap the number of published pages and usually leave the app's own branding visible on your storefront, which looks unpolished to shoppers. Most stores that stick with a builder past the testing phase end up on a paid tier within the first few months.
