What to Look for in a Shopify Developer Review

Online reviews — on Upwork, Fiverr, LinkedIn, or personal websites — tell you a story, but you have to know how to read it. Quantity matters less than specificity. A developer with 200 generic 5-star reviews ("great work, fast delivery") tells you almost nothing. Five reviews that describe specific technical problems solved, measurable outcomes achieved, and positive communication throughout tell you a great deal.

When reading reviews, look for: mentions of specific technical work (custom Liquid sections, app integration, speed optimization), measurable results (conversion rate improved, traffic increased, load time reduced), timelines met (delivered on schedule, responsive to revisions), and repeat engagements (clients who hired the same developer multiple times are the strongest signal of genuine quality).

Also look at the developer's response to any negative reviews. A professional who acknowledges issues, explains what happened, and demonstrates accountability is more trustworthy than someone with a suspiciously perfect record. Every developer who has worked on enough projects has had a difficult client situation — how they handle it matters.

How to Check a Portfolio — Live Stores, GitHub, Code Quality

Live Stores

This is the most important portfolio check. Do not accept screenshots only. Ask for live URLs and actually visit the stores. Run each store through PageSpeed Insights (pagespeed.web.dev) and check the mobile score. Browse the product pages, add to cart, and evaluate the checkout flow. Are the store's custom features working smoothly? Does the design look intentional or like a template with minimal changes? Is the mobile UX polished?

Also inspect the store's theme code — right-click, View Page Source, look at the Liquid/JavaScript structure. While you may not be able to evaluate code quality directly, the structure reveals something: are there excessive third-party scripts? Is the code organized or chaotic? Does the page weight seem reasonable?

GitHub Profile

A serious Shopify developer will have a GitHub profile with some public repositories — theme components, Shopify app starters, or custom tools. The absence of any GitHub activity is not automatically a red flag (many client projects are private), but public repos demonstrate technical thinking and give you concrete code to evaluate. Look for: clean commits with meaningful messages, use of Shopify CLI, modern JavaScript practices, and Liquid that follows best practices.

Red Flags That Should End the Conversation

The Shopify developer market has a significant number of people who claim expertise they don't have. Here are the definitive red flags:

  • No live store URLs — Screenshots can be faked or from purchased themes. If a developer can't provide a single live store you can visit and test, walk away.
  • Generic screenshots from free themes — If their "portfolio" is screenshots of Dawn or Debut with minor color changes, that's a theme installer, not a developer.
  • No GitHub or coding presence — Combined with no live stores, this strongly suggests no real coding is being done.
  • Promises that seem impossible — "I'll build your full custom Shopify store in 24 hours for $50." The math doesn't work. Either the quality is garbage or the project will be abandoned.
  • Inability to explain their process — Ask a developer to explain how they would approach a specific technical problem. Vague or copied-from-Shopify-docs answers reveal shallow knowledge.
  • No revision or bug-fix policy — Any developer who won't commit to fixing bugs in code they wrote is someone you don't want to work with.
  • Upfront full payment demands — Professional Shopify developers typically work in milestones (50% upfront, 50% on delivery) or use platforms like Upwork with escrow protection.

Questions to Ask Before Hiring

These five questions separate genuine Shopify developers from imposters:

  1. "Can you walk me through how you'd implement a custom Liquid section with metafields?" — A real developer will describe the JSON schema, the Liquid rendering logic, and section settings. Someone guessing will be vague.
  2. "How do you handle theme updates without losing customizations?" — The answer should involve version control (Git), a child theme or backup process, and a documented change log.
  3. "What's your approach to Shopify page speed optimization?" — Look for specific answers: lazy loading, app script auditing, image compression pipeline, critical CSS, and avoiding render-blocking resources.
  4. "Show me a specific problem you solved on a recent project." — You want a technical story with a challenge, an approach, and a measurable outcome.
  5. "What are your communication and revision policies?" — Clear, professional answers indicate someone who takes client relationships seriously.

Developer Vetting Checklist

  • 3+ live store URLs provided and tested
  • PageSpeed Insights scores reviewed (target: 80+ mobile)
  • GitHub profile with relevant activity
  • Specific technical questions answered confidently
  • Client reviews that mention specific technical work
  • Clear revision and bug-fix policy stated
  • Milestone-based payment structure offered
  • Responsive communication during initial inquiry
  • Honest timeline estimates (not "I'll finish in 48 hours")
