Shopify vs Amazon: An Honest Comparison for Sellers in 2026

This isn't really a fair fight — Shopify and Amazon solve different problems. Amazon hands you a river of ready-to-buy shoppers in exchange for control and fees. Shopify hands you full control and ownership in exchange for having to generate every visitor yourself. Most sellers who understand this end up using both, not choosing one forever.

The Core Trade-off: Traffic vs Ownership

Amazon's entire value proposition is demand that already exists. Shoppers arrive on Amazon actively looking to buy, and the platform's search and recommendation engine puts your product in front of them without you spending a cent on awareness marketing. The cost of that convenience is that Amazon owns the customer relationship — you typically don't get the buyer's email address, you can't build a mailing list from Amazon orders, and Amazon can (and does) launch competing private-label products once it sees a category selling well.

Shopify flips this completely. You own every customer record, every email address, every piece of behavioral data, and you can retarget, upsell, and build a brand relationship over years. The cost is that none of that traffic exists until you build it — a new Shopify store gets zero organic visitors on day one, full stop.

Net effect: Amazon's all-in cost is usually higher per unit sold because of referral and FBA fees stacking, but it can require less upfront marketing spend to get that first sale. Shopify's per-unit cost is lower, but you're funding 100% of your own traffic. Run both scenarios with your actual product cost and category fee before deciding — the gap between the two varies a lot by category and average order value.

Control Over Pricing, Design, and Brand

On Amazon, your listing lives inside Amazon's template — same layout, same fonts, same "customers also bought" module often showing a competitor right next to your buy box. Price too far above competitors selling the same or similar product and you lose the buy box entirely, regardless of your brand story.

On Shopify, the storefront is entirely yours: layout, photography direction, bundling, loyalty programs, and pricing strategy (including psychological pricing, tiered discounts, and subscription models) are all under your control. This is the main reason brand-focused sellers eventually move toward Shopify — Amazon is built for commodity discovery, not brand storytelling.

Fulfillment and Logistics: FBA vs Doing It Yourself

Amazon FBA (Fulfillment by Amazon) is genuinely one of the platform's strongest advantages — Amazon stores, packs, ships, and handles most customer service and returns for a per-unit fee. For sellers without warehouse space or fulfillment experience, this removes an entire operational headache and, as a side benefit, FBA products qualify for Prime shipping, which meaningfully boosts conversion since so many Amazon shoppers filter for Prime-eligible items only.

Shopify gives you no equivalent built-in fulfillment network by default — you're choosing between self-fulfillment, a third-party logistics (3PL) provider, or Shopify's own fulfillment network where available. This means more setup work upfront, but also more flexibility: custom packaging, branded inserts, and control over exactly how and when orders ship, none of which Amazon's standardized fulfillment allows.

Reviews and Trust Signals

Amazon's review system is mature, heavily trusted by shoppers, and portable in the sense that a product's reviews stay with the listing regardless of who's selling it (for shared listings). But that same review pool is visible to every competing offer on the same listing, and a single quality issue can tank a product's rating fast, with limited recourse. Shopify has no built-in review system — you install a reviews app and build trust signals yourself through testimonials, UGC, and third-party badges (Trustpilot, Google reviews). It takes longer to build credibility from zero, but the trust you build belongs entirely to your brand rather than a shared marketplace listing.

Traffic: Built-In Demand vs Traffic You Must Generate

Be honest with yourself about which situation you're actually in. If you have no existing audience, no ad budget, and a product in a well-searched category, Amazon's built-in demand can get you to your first sales far faster than a brand-new Shopify store with zero traffic. If you already have an audience (social following, email list, existing customers from another channel) or plan to invest seriously in content, SEO, or paid social, Shopify lets you capture 100% of the value of that traffic instead of sharing it with a marketplace.

When to Use Both Together

The most common real-world setup among established sellers isn't "Shopify vs Amazon" — it's both, used for different jobs. Amazon serves as a discovery and volume channel, often priced slightly higher to protect margin after fees. Shopify serves as the brand home: better margins (no referral fee), an owned customer list, subscription and loyalty programs, and a place to sell bundles or exclusives Amazon's listing format can't easily support. Keep SKUs and photography assets consistent across both, but treat pricing and promotions as separate strategies since the fee structures are so different.

Migration Considerations

Moving from Amazon-only to a Shopify-plus-Amazon setup usually means rebuilding product content from scratch — Amazon bullet points and Shopify product descriptions serve different purposes and shouldn't just be copy-pasted. Budget time for proper SEO-friendly product pages, a returns/refund policy that matches your own terms rather than Amazon's, and a plan for driving that first wave of non-Amazon traffic (email to existing Amazon reviewers isn't allowed, so this really does start near zero). If you're migrating an existing e-commerce presence onto Shopify more broadly, our Shopify migration guide covers the technical side in detail.

Watch for a few practical traps sellers underestimate: Amazon's Terms of Service restrict including any off-Amazon marketing material (discount codes, URLs, QR codes) inside FBA packaging, so you can't simply insert a "shop our Shopify store" card into Amazon orders. Building your Shopify audience from an Amazon base has to happen through indirect means — social content, SEO, and paid ads — rather than direct package inserts. Also budget real time for photography and copywriting: Amazon-optimized images (often with infographic overlays) don't translate well to a clean Shopify product page, and vice versa.

Being Fair to Amazon: What It Genuinely Does Better

It's easy to write an Amazon-vs-Shopify comparison that quietly favors Shopify throughout, but that wouldn't be honest. Amazon's logistics network, established buyer trust, one-click purchasing, and sheer existing search volume are advantages that took Amazon two decades and enormous capital to build — no individual seller replicates that by switching platforms. For commodity products, gifts, and impulse purchases where brand loyalty matters less than availability and price, Amazon often converts better than a standalone store ever will, regardless of how well that store is built. The right question isn't "which platform is better" — it's "which job does each platform do better for my specific product and goals."

International reach is another area where Amazon has a genuine head start: its regional marketplaces (Amazon.de, Amazon.co.uk, Amazon.ca, and others) give a seller instant access to established local buyer trust in each country, something a new Shopify store has to build market by market. If global reach matters early, that's a real point in Amazon's favor worth weighing against the ownership benefits Shopify provides.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it cheaper to sell on Shopify or Amazon?

Per-unit fees are usually lower on Shopify since you avoid Amazon's referral and FBA fees, but Shopify requires you to fund your own traffic, which Amazon partially provides for free through marketplace search.

Can I sell the same products on both platforms?

Yes, and many established brands do exactly this — Amazon for volume and discovery, Shopify for margin and brand relationship. Just keep pricing strategy and inventory management deliberately separate, and be mindful of minimum advertised price policies if you run wholesale or other channels alongside both.

Should a brand-new seller start on Amazon or Shopify?

If you have zero existing audience and need fast validation, Amazon's built-in demand can get you initial sales data faster. If you already have an audience or plan to invest in content/ads, starting on Shopify lets you keep full margin and customer ownership from day one.
