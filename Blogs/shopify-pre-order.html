Pre-Order vs Backorder vs Coming Soon

These three terms are often used interchangeably but represent meaningfully different customer experiences and operational setups. Choosing the right model for your situation determines your messaging, your app selection, and your fulfillment workflow.

Pre-Order

A product that has never been in stock — customers are ordering in advance of a product that is yet to be manufactured, shipped, or received. Pre-orders are appropriate when you have confirmed the product exists and have a reasonable estimate of when it will be available. The key communication requirement: a specific or approximate expected ship date displayed prominently on the product page and in all order communications.

Backorder

A product that was previously in stock and has sold out, but is expected to be restocked. The distinction from pre-order matters because customers already know the product — they may have considered it before, seen reviews, or even purchased it previously. Backorder messaging should acknowledge the situation clearly: "This item is temporarily out of stock — estimated restock [date]. Order now to secure your place in the fulfillment queue."

Coming Soon

A product in development or not yet available for purchase — customers can register interest but cannot yet complete a transaction. Coming Soon pages collect email leads (using Klaviyo or Mailchimp integration) and build a launch list before the product goes live. The transition from Coming Soon to Pre-Order to In-Stock should be seamless — ideally automated using product status and Shopify Flow triggers.

Pre-Order Product Page Best Practices

The product page is where pre-order trust is made or broken. Customers are being asked to pay for something they cannot immediately receive — every element of the page must address the implicit anxiety this creates. Unclear messaging, buried shipping estimates, or no explanation of the pre-order process all increase cart abandonment and post-purchase cancellation requests.

Clear Estimated Ship Date

Display the estimated ship date or date range prominently — above the fold, before the Add to Cart button. Use plain language: "Ships approximately June 15, 2026" or "Expected delivery: late June 2026." If your estimate has uncertainty built in, acknowledge it: "Expected to ship in 3–5 weeks — we will notify you of any changes." Vague language like "ships soon" creates disproportionate negative reactions when expectations are not met.

Transparent Payment Handling

If you're charging at order (the most common approach), state this clearly: "Your card will be charged today. Your order will ship when stock arrives." If you're using a deposit model, explain both payments: "Pay 25% today ($9.99) to reserve your item. The remaining 75% ($29.99) will be charged when your order ships." Surprises around payment timing generate chargebacks — eliminate them with proactive transparency.

Inline FAQ on the Product Page

Add a collapsible FAQ section directly on the pre-order product page addressing: when will it ship, what happens if delayed, how do I cancel, can I combine with in-stock items, and when will my card be charged. This FAQ eliminates the top 5 support tickets for pre-order products and increases conversion by addressing objections at the decision point.

Managing Pre-Order Fulfillment & Communication

The customer relationship during the pre-order waiting period is as important as the initial sale. Silence breeds anxiety and cancellation requests. A proactive communication sequence keeps customers informed, reduces inbound support, and builds brand trust that converts pre-order customers into repeat buyers.

Email Sequence Design

Design a 3-part automated email sequence: (1) Order confirmation with expected ship date, FAQs, and a note on what happens next. (2) Status update email sent 2 weeks before expected ship date — confirming the timeline or communicating any changes. (3) Shipping confirmation when the order is fulfilled, with tracking details. Apps like Timesact have this sequence built in; for Klaviyo users, build a custom flow triggered by the "pre-order" tag on the order.

Pre-Order Communication Timeline

  • Immediately: Order confirmation with expected ship date and payment summary
  • If delayed: Proactive delay notification within 24 hours of learning about the delay
  • 2 weeks before expected ship: Fulfillment update and excitement re-engagement email
  • When shipped: Dispatch confirmation with tracking number
  • 3 days after delivery: Review request and cross-sell email

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I charge customers at pre-order or at shipment? +
Charging at pre-order provides immediate cash flow and firm demand signals to inform your inventory order quantities, but creates legal and reputational risk if you cannot fulfill within the stated timeframe. Charging at shipment reduces conversion rate (customers are less committed) but eliminates the risk of holding customer payments. For timelines under 4 weeks with high confidence, charging at order is standard. For longer or uncertain timelines, charge at shipment or use a partial deposit model.
Which Shopify plan do I need for pre-orders? +
Pre-order apps work on all Shopify plans including Basic ($39/month). There is no plan tier restriction for enabling pre-orders. The "continue selling when out of stock" inventory setting required for pre-orders is available on all plans. Advanced features like partial payment (deposit model) at pre-order require apps with specific payment gateway compatibility — Timesact and Purple Dot support this on all plans.
How do I handle mixed carts with pre-order and in-stock items? +
Mixed carts are the most common pre-order implementation challenge. The best solution is automatic order splitting — in-stock items ship immediately as a separate fulfillment, and the pre-order items are held for fulfillment when stock arrives. Both Timesact and PreOrder Now handle this automatically. The customer receives two separate shipping notifications. Some stores prefer to block mixed carts entirely (requiring separate checkouts), which simplifies operations at the cost of conversion rate.
What if a customer wants to cancel their pre-order? +
Always allow pre-order cancellations — especially when you charged at the time of ordering. In most jurisdictions, customers have the right to cancel unshipped orders. Your pre-order policy should clearly state the cancellation window and process, with a simple email request mechanism. Handling cancellations gracefully — fast refunds, no friction — converts a potentially negative experience into brand loyalty and prevents chargebacks.
Are there legal obligations around pre-orders? +
Yes, significant ones. In the US, the FTC's Mail, Internet, or Telephone Order Rule requires shipping within the stated timeframe or notifying customers of delays with the option to cancel and receive a full refund. UK Consumer Rights Act and Distance Selling Regulations apply similar protections. EU Consumer Rights Directive governs pre-orders with strict cancellation rights. Core obligations across all markets: clearly state estimated delivery, notify customers immediately of delays, and offer full refunds if you cannot fulfill.
