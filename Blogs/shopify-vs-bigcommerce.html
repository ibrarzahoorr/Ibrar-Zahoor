Shopify vs BigCommerce 2026

I build almost exclusively on Shopify, so it would be easy to write this as a one-sided pitch. I'm not going to — BigCommerce has genuine, structural advantages for a specific type of business, and pretending otherwise doesn't help you make the right call. Pricing Compared

Both platforms use tiered monthly plans, but the way costs actually grow as your business scales differs in an important way.

Growth planAdvanced, ~$399/moPro, ~$399/mo (or more if sales exceed threshold)
EnterpriseShopify Plus, custom (~$2,300+/mo)Enterprise, fully custom quote

The sticker prices at the entry and mid tiers now look nearly identical — that part of the "Shopify is cheaper" narrative is outdated. The real difference is in how each platform scales: BigCommerce's Standard, Plus, and Pro plans have revenue-based upgrade thresholds — cross an annual online sales limit and BigCommerce pushes you to the next plan regardless of whether you actually need the extra features. Shopify's plans are flat regardless of your sales volume; you upgrade only when you want a feature, not because you sold too much. For a fast-growing store, that's a real practical difference worth planning around.

Transaction Fees: BigCommerce's Real Advantage

This is the one area where BigCommerce has an unambiguous, structural edge, and it's worth stating plainly: BigCommerce never charges an additional transaction fee, no matter which payment gateway you use. Shopify does — unless you use Shopify Payments, every other gateway (Authorize.net, PayPal alongside another primary processor, various regional processors) triggers an extra 0.5-2% fee on top of whatever the gateway itself charges, with the exact percentage depending on your plan tier.

This matters more than it sounds like on paper. Shopify Payments isn't available in every country — Pakistan, where I'm based, is one of the markets without it, so merchants here pay Shopify's extra transaction fee regardless of plan. The same is true for merchants in a number of other countries, and for any business that needs a specific enterprise gateway Shopify Payments doesn't support. If that's your situation, BigCommerce's fee structure is a genuine, ongoing cost saving — not a marketing footnote.

App Ecosystem: Where Shopify Pulls Far Ahead

The Shopify App Store carries well over 10,000 listed apps; the BigCommerce Marketplace sits closer to 1,000. In practice that gap shows up in two ways: first, obscure or brand-new marketing tools (a new BNPL provider, a niche loyalty platform, a trending TikTok integration) reliably ship a Shopify app first and a BigCommerce one months later, if at all. Second, competition among Shopify apps in any given category (reviews, upsells, subscriptions) keeps pricing and feature quality more competitive, since there are more players fighting for the same merchants.

BigCommerce partly offsets this with more capability built into the core platform itself (see below), so you need fewer apps to begin with — but for anything genuinely niche, Shopify's marketplace depth is a real advantage.

Built-In Features: BigCommerce Ships More Out of the Box

This is where BigCommerce earns real credit. Multi-currency display, more flexible product filtering/faceted search, native multiple storefronts, and considerably stronger built-in B2B functionality (customer groups, negotiated price lists, quote requests) all come standard on BigCommerce plans where Shopify would require you to bolt on apps — apps that cost money and add points of failure. A B2B or wholesale-heavy business often needs meaningfully fewer paid apps to reach the same functionality on BigCommerce than on Shopify.

Shopify's answer to this is Shopify Plus, which adds native B2B tooling, multiple storefronts (via Shopify Markets and B2B channels), and more built-in flexibility — but that's an enterprise-tier plan starting around $2,300/month, not something available on Shopify's standard plans the way it is on BigCommerce's mid tier.

Theme & Design Flexibility

Shopify's Liquid templating language has a far larger developer community, a bigger library of free and premium themes, and — practically speaking for you as a business owner — a much larger pool of freelancers and agencies who can customise it. BigCommerce's Stencil framework is capable, but the talent pool is smaller and ready-made themes are fewer, which can mean higher costs and longer timelines for a fully custom design.

Where BigCommerce claws some of this back is in how much default functionality its themes expose without custom code — a merchant doing straightforward product display and filtering may need less custom theme work on BigCommerce than the equivalent Shopify build. For anything visually ambitious or brand-heavy, Shopify's larger talent pool and app-store design tools generally get you there faster and cheaper.

Headless Commerce: Both Are Legitimate Now

This used to be a clearer BigCommerce win — its API-first architecture and native GraphQL support gave it a head start on headless builds years before Shopify had an equivalent story. That gap has closed. Shopify's Hydrogen (a React framework) paired with Oxygen hosting is now a mature, well-documented path to a fully headless storefront, backed by a large ecosystem of examples and community support. BigCommerce's Catalyst (a Next.js-based, open-source starter) is a legitimate competitor and appeals to teams who specifically want an open-source foundation rather than a vendor-hosted framework.

Neither platform has a decisive edge here anymore — the right choice mostly comes down to which platform's underlying commerce features and cost structure suit your business, since the headless layer on both is genuinely capable.

Who Should Actually Choose Which

Choose BigCommerce if: you run a B2B/wholesale business that needs native customer groups and price lists without stacking apps; you process meaningful volume through a payment gateway other than Shopify Payments and the transaction-fee savings genuinely add up; you're a mid-market or enterprise merchant who wants more commerce functionality built into the core platform rather than assembled from third-party apps; or you specifically want an open-source headless foundation via Catalyst.

Choose Shopify if: you're a DTC brand that wants the fastest path to launch with the largest available selection of themes, apps, and freelance/agency talent; you're in (or plan to expand into) a country where Shopify Payments is available and want to avoid extra fees entirely; you expect to scale into enterprise territory and want Shopify Plus's B2B, multi-store, and checkout customisation ecosystem; or you simply want the biggest, most actively maintained app marketplace for whatever marketing or operations tool you'll need next year that doesn't exist yet today.

Neither platform is objectively "better" — they're optimised for different shapes of business, and the honest answer for most merchants comes down to whether built-in B2B/fee structure (BigCommerce) or ecosystem depth and talent availability (Shopify) matters more for how you actually operate.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is BigCommerce actually cheaper than Shopify?

Not consistently. Entry and mid-tier list prices are now close to identical. BigCommerce can end up cheaper if you'd otherwise pay Shopify's extra transaction fee, but can end up costing more if your sales growth pushes you past its revenue-based plan thresholds sooner than you'd naturally want to upgrade.

Can I migrate from BigCommerce to Shopify without losing my SEO rankings?

Yes, with care — proper 301 redirects from old BigCommerce URLs to new Shopify URLs, matching metadata, and a submitted updated sitemap are the core steps. It's a well-understood migration process, not a rare or risky one.

Which platform is better for a growing B2B/wholesale business?

Out of the box, BigCommerce's native customer groups and price lists give it an edge on Standard/Plus plans. At real scale, Shopify Plus's B2B tooling closes most of that gap — so the answer depends on whether you're a mid-market business today or building toward enterprise scale.
