10 Criteria to Choose the Right Shopify Developer

Use this checklist for every developer you evaluate — a low score on any criterion is a deal-breaker.

1

Live Portfolio of Shopify Stores

Ask for URLs to real, live Shopify stores they built. Test them on mobile and desktop. A strong developer has 5+ live stores — no portfolio = immediate red flag.

2

Liquid Programming Expertise

Liquid is Shopify's templating language — every custom feature requires it. Ask them to explain how they'd build a custom product filter in Liquid. Vague answers reveal shallow skills.

3

Shopify Partner Status

Official Shopify Partners have certified training and partner dashboard access. Ask for their Partner Dashboard link to verify. This signals real, sustained investment in the platform.

4

Relevant Industry Experience

Ask specifically: "Have you built a store in my industry?" A developer who's handled your product type understands your customer journey, compliance needs, and conversion patterns.

5

Clear, Detailed Written Proposal

Expect: deliverables, timeline, milestones, revision policy, and payment schedule. Vague proposals lead to scope creep. No written proposal means no accountability.

6

Strong English Communication

Miscommunication is the #1 cause of failed projects. Email them a technical question and evaluate the clarity of their reply. Unclear writing = unclear code and deliverables.

7

Verifiable Client Testimonials

Ask for 2–3 references with contact info. Check Upwork, LinkedIn, and Google reviews. AI-generated testimonials exist — insist on contacts you can reach independently.

8

Transparent, Market-Rate Pricing

Rates: $15–$50/hr (Pakistan/Asia), $50–$100/hr (Eastern Europe), $80–$200/hr (USA/UK). Both suspiciously cheap ($5/hr) and overpriced ($300/hr for basic work) are red flags.

9

Post-Launch Support Policy

What happens if something breaks 3 weeks after launch? Quality developers offer 2–4 weeks of free bug fixes post-launch. Get this in writing before you pay anything.

10

You Own All Code and Documentation

Ask: "Can another developer modify this code after delivery?" You must own clean, documented code. Proprietary black boxes lock you into dependency on one developer forever.

8 Red Flags That Expose Bad Shopify Developers

  • No live portfolio links — only screenshots or Figma mockups, never real URLs
  • Unrealistically fast timelines — "I'll build your full custom store in 2 days"
  • Asks for 100% upfront payment — legitimate developers use milestone-based payments
  • Can't answer specific technical questions — vague or rehearsed responses signal copied portfolios
  • No written agreement — "we'll figure it out as we go" ends in disputes
  • Calls a purchased theme "custom development" — ask if they're modifying a bought theme vs. building from scratch
  • No revision rounds included — quality developers include at least 2 revision rounds
  • Slow pre-sales communication — unresponsive before payment = invisible after payment

7 Interview Questions to Ask Any Shopify Developer

1. "Walk me through a complex Liquid build you've completed."

Listen for specifics: metafields, custom sections, conditional logic, AJAX cart. Generic answers reveal shallow experience.

2. "How do you handle SEO during a new store build?"

They should mention: canonical URLs, schema markup, sitemap, image alt text, mobile-first indexing, and page speed targets.

3. "What happens if the project takes longer than estimated?"

Listen for milestone agreements and transparent communication. Reputable developers own delays they caused.

4. "Have you migrated a store from WooCommerce or Magento to Shopify?"

Experience shows they've handled product data, customer records, SEO redirects, and payment setups without data loss.

5. "Can you show a store where you improved the conversion rate?"

Business-focused developers track results. They should cite before/after data or clearly explain UX decisions they made.

6. "What PageSpeed score do your stores typically achieve?"

Target: 80+ mobile, 90+ desktop. Developers who've never checked PageSpeed are not performance-aware.

7. "How will you communicate project progress?"

Expect: weekly updates, a project tool (Trello/Notion), and agreed milestones. "I'll message when done" is not a process.
