Shopify vs Squarespace 2026: Honest Comparison for Store Owners

Squarespace and Shopify get compared constantly, but most articles pick a winner and force-fit every business into it. The honest answer is that both platforms are excellent at what they were built for — and mediocre at the other's job. Here's what actually separates them, without the marketing spin.

Two Platforms Built for Different Jobs

Squarespace started as a website and portfolio builder, and it still shows — its templates are genuinely some of the best-looking on the web, its editor is drag-and-drop simple, and it bundles a blog, booking system, and basic store into one flat monthly fee. Shopify started as an ecommerce checkout system and grew a website builder around it. That origin story explains almost every difference below.

If you're a photographer, coach, consultant, or restaurant owner who wants a beautiful site with occasional product sales, Squarespace's design-first approach will get you further, faster, with less fuss. If products and transactions are the actual business — not a side feature of it — Shopify's ecommerce engine is built for volume, growth, and complexity in a way Squarespace simply isn't.

The honest rule of thumb: if you'd describe your site as "a website that also sells things," lean Squarespace. If you'd describe it as "a store," lean Shopify. Most founders who guess wrong pick Squarespace for a business that's about to scale past its ceiling.

Checkout and Payments: Where the Gap Is Widest

This is the single biggest differentiator and the one most comparison articles gloss over. Squarespace Commerce checkout is functional but narrow: Stripe and PayPal are effectively your only options, there's no native abandoned-checkout automation on lower plans, and customizing the checkout flow beyond Squarespace's fixed steps isn't possible — there's no checkout API to hook into.

Shopify's checkout, by contrast, is one of the highest-converting checkout flows in ecommerce, benchmarked across millions of stores. It supports Shop Pay one-click payments, over 100 payment gateways depending on region, dynamic tax and duty calculation for international orders, and — on Shopify Plus — a fully scriptable checkout extensibility framework for custom logic (BOGO rules, gated discounts, post-purchase upsells). If your store sells internationally, offers financing options like Shop Pay Installments or Klarna, or needs checkout customization for a specific promotion, Squarespace cannot follow you there.

App Ecosystem: The Gap That Compounds

Squarespace's extension directory covers the basics — email marketing, reviews, a handful of shipping tools. Shopify's app store has over 13,000 apps covering everything from subscription billing to loyalty programs to complex B2B wholesale portals. This matters less on day one and matters enormously by month six, once you realize you need a specific feature — say, product bundles, a wholesale price list, or a custom quiz-based product recommender — and discover Squarespace simply has no path to build it.

Shopify's app ecosystem also means you're rarely stuck waiting on the platform vendor to ship a feature. If Shopify itself doesn't support something natively, there's almost always a well-reviewed app — or a developer can build a custom one using Shopify's public APIs and Liquid templating. Squarespace's closed system means you're limited to what Squarespace itself decides to build.

SEO: Closer Than People Assume, But Not Equal

Squarespace actually deserves credit here — its templates are clean, semantic, and mobile-responsive by default, and basic on-page SEO (meta titles, descriptions, alt text, clean URLs) is fully supported. For a small content-driven site, Squarespace's out-of-the-box SEO is genuinely competitive.

Shopify pulls ahead at scale for two reasons. First, Shopify's Liquid templating gives developers granular control over structured data, canonical tags, and page-speed optimization that Squarespace's more rigid template system doesn't expose. Second, ecommerce SEO increasingly depends on things Squarespace handles weakly — faceted collection filtering, large-catalog sitemap generation, and product schema markup that feeds Google Shopping and rich snippets. A 30-product Squarespace store won't feel the difference. A 3,000-SKU catalog will.

Pricing: Squarespace Looks Cheaper Until You Add Apps

Squarespace's Commerce plans (roughly $23–$49/month billed annually) look cheaper than Shopify's Basic and Grow tiers ($29–$105/month) at first glance, and Squarespace's flat pricing with no required apps can genuinely stay cheaper for a simple store. But Shopify stores that need extra functionality are paying for named apps that solve one problem well, while Squarespace stores that outgrow the built-in feature set often have no equivalent to pay for at all — the ceiling isn't a pricing tier, it's a hard capability wall.

Transaction fees matter too: Squarespace charges no extra transaction fee if you use Stripe/PayPal, similar to Shopify Payments. But Shopify's third-party payment surcharge (1–2% if you skip Shopify Payments) doesn't have a Squarespace equivalent to compare against, since Squarespace's gateway options are already limited to two providers.

Finding Help When You Outgrow the Basics

An underrated factor in this comparison is the size of the talent pool behind each platform. Shopify's popularity has produced a large, mature market of theme developers, Shopify Partners, and specialized agencies who can build almost anything on top of the platform — custom checkout logic, complex subscription models, headless storefronts, ERP integrations. If you need something Shopify doesn't do natively, there's very likely a developer who's built it before.

Squarespace's smaller developer ecosystem means custom work is harder to source and typically more expensive relative to the platform's simpler feature set — partly because there's less demand for deep Squarespace customization, and partly because the platform's closed template system limits how much a developer can actually change. For a business that expects to need ongoing technical help as it grows, this gap in available expertise is worth factoring in alongside the platform's own feature set.

Design Templates and Customization Depth

It's worth being fair to Squarespace here: its templates are arguably the best-looking, most cohesive designs available out of the box on any website builder, and for a brand that lives or dies on visual polish with minimal ecommerce complexity, that matters. Shopify's free themes (Dawn and its Online Store 2.0 siblings) are solid and fast but generally need more deliberate design work to reach the same immediate visual impact.

Where Shopify pulls ahead is customization ceiling. Squarespace templates are attractive but relatively rigid — you're styling within a fixed structure. Shopify's Liquid templating and section/block architecture let a developer rebuild almost any part of the storefront: custom product page layouts, unique collection filtering, bespoke checkout branding on Plus. If your brand needs a genuinely distinctive storefront rather than a well-designed template, Shopify's ceiling is much higher, at the cost of needing more deliberate design effort to get there.

Who Should Actually Choose Each Platform

1

Choose Squarespace If

You're a service business, creator, or portfolio site where products are secondary — a coach selling one digital course, a photographer with a print shop, a restaurant with a small merch table. You value design polish and simplicity over ecommerce depth, and you're unlikely to need more than 20-30 SKUs.

2

Choose Shopify If

Products are the business, not a feature of it. You plan to scale past a handful of SKUs, sell internationally, run promotions and discount logic, integrate email/SMS marketing platforms, or eventually need wholesale/B2B, subscriptions, or a custom-built storefront. Shopify's ceiling is dramatically higher and its migration cost, once you're established, is real.

3

Migrating Later Is Painful Either Way

Store owners who start on Squarespace and outgrow it face a full replatform — product data, orders, SEO redirects, and customer accounts don't move over cleanly. If there's any realistic chance ecommerce becomes the main event, starting on Shopify avoids a costly rebuild later.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sell physical products on Squarespace at all?

Yes — Squarespace Commerce plans support physical products, inventory tracking, and basic shipping rules. It's a real ecommerce option for small catalogs; it just doesn't scale the way Shopify does once your catalog, order volume, or feature needs grow.

Is Shopify harder to learn than Squarespace?

Slightly, yes — Squarespace's editor is more visual and beginner-friendly out of the box. Shopify's admin has a steeper initial learning curve but is arguably more logical once you understand its structure, and most merchants are comfortable within a week.

Can I migrate from Squarespace to Shopify later without losing SEO?

Yes, with care. You'll need to map old URLs to new ones with 301 redirects, rebuild product and collection pages, and resubmit your sitemap to Google Search Console. Done properly, most stores retain the bulk of their organic rankings within a few months.
