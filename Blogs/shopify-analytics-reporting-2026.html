Shopify Analytics & Reporting 2026: Dashboards, ShopifyQL & KPIs

Most merchants check three or four numbers in Shopify and call it "analytics" — sales today, orders, maybe traffic. That leaves most of the useful data on the table. Here's how to actually use Shopify's native reporting stack, ShopifyQL, and GA4 together to make decisions instead of just watching numbers.

This guide covers what's built into Shopify natively, how to extend it with GA4 and third-party apps, and — most importantly — which numbers actually deserve your attention on a day-to-day, week-to-week, and quarter-to-quarter basis.

What's Actually in Shopify's Native Analytics

Shopify Analytics (under Analytics > Reports in the admin) ships with dashboards covering sales, orders, customers, marketing, retention, inventory, and behavior. Basic and Grow plans get the core sales/traffic overview; deeper reports (cohort analysis, custom report building, ShopifyQL editor) are gated to Shopify and Advanced-tier plans and above.

  • Sales reports break down revenue by product, channel, discount code, and location — useful for spotting which acquisition channel or product line is actually profitable, not just busy.
  • Behavior reports show sessions, conversion rate by device and traffic source, and where in the funnel visitors drop off — critical for diagnosing whether a traffic or a conversion problem is hurting revenue.
  • Customer reports cover new vs. returning customer split, cohort retention, and average order value by segment — the numbers that actually tell you if you're building a sustainable business or constantly refilling a leaky bucket.
  • Inventory reports track sell-through rate, days of stock remaining, and dead stock — genuinely underused by merchants who focus purely on sales dashboards and only look at inventory when they're already out of stock on a bestseller.
Common mistake: obsessing over daily sales while ignoring the returning-customer rate. A store with flat daily sales but rising repeat-purchase rate is usually healthier than one with growing sales driven entirely by new-customer ad spend.

It's also worth building the habit of checking reports at the right cadence for what they measure — sales and traffic are fine to glance at daily, but retention, LTV, and inventory turnover are slow-moving metrics that need weekly or monthly windows to show a meaningful trend. Checking a slow metric daily mostly just adds noise and anxiety without giving you anything actionable to change.

ShopifyQL: Writing Custom Reports

ShopifyQL is Shopify's own query language for building custom reports directly against your store's data, available through the analytics editor on qualifying plans. It uses a readable, SQL-like syntax rather than requiring a full database background, which makes it accessible to merchants who aren't developers but still want more than the canned reports allow.

1

Start from a template

The ShopifyQL editor includes starter queries for common questions (sales by product over time, order count by discount code) — clone one and adjust the fields rather than writing from scratch.

2

Filter to the actual question

Add a WHERE-style filter for date range, sales channel, or product type so the report answers one specific question — "which collection drove the most first-time-customer revenue in Q1" — rather than showing everything at once.

3

Visualize and save

Choose a chart type (line, bar, table) that matches the question, then save the report to a custom dashboard so you're not rebuilding the same query weekly.

4

Share with your team

Custom dashboards can be shared with staff accounts with reporting-only permissions, so your team sees the same numbers without needing full admin access.

The learning curve for ShopifyQL is genuinely gentle compared to writing raw SQL against a database export — most merchants can build a useful custom report within an hour of experimenting with the editor. Where it pays off most is combining fields the canned reports never let you cross — for example, sales by product filtered to only first-time customers, which tells you what's actually driving new customer acquisition rather than what your existing base already loves.

Connecting GA4 to Shopify

Shopify's native reports are excellent for store-side data (orders, products, checkout) but weak on the marketing-attribution side — understanding which specific ad, keyword, or landing page brought a visitor in the first place. That's where Google Analytics 4 fills the gap, tracking full-funnel behavior including pre-purchase browsing across sessions and devices.

The cleanest setup uses Shopify's native Google & YouTube channel integration to pass enhanced ecommerce events (view_item, add_to_cart, purchase) into GA4 automatically, rather than relying on a manually pasted tracking snippet that tends to double-count or miss events after theme updates. Cohort and LTV Analysis in Practice

A cohort report groups customers by when they first purchased (say, by month) and tracks how much that group spends over the following months — the single best tool for answering "is this business actually getting healthier over time." Most merchants skip this because the raw report can look intimidating; in practice, you only need to watch two lines.

1

Pull a 6-12 month cohort view

Group customers by first-purchase month and track cumulative revenue per cohort over the following months. Rising later-cohort curves mean retention is improving; flattening curves mean you're increasingly dependent on one-time buyers.

2

Calculate a rough LTV per acquisition channel

Segment cohorts by acquisition source where possible — paid social customers often have a very different repeat-purchase curve than organic search or referral customers, which should directly change how much you're willing to spend to acquire each type.

3

Compare LTV to acquisition cost

Once you have a rough LTV per channel, weigh it against your blended customer acquisition cost from ad platforms. A channel with a low LTV-to-CAC ratio is quietly losing money even if the "cost per purchase" number looks fine in isolation.

4

Re-run quarterly, not just once

Cohort behavior shifts with seasonality, pricing changes, and product mix — a single snapshot from launch tells you little six months later. Build this into a recurring quarterly review rather than a one-time analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need GA4 if I already have Shopify Analytics?

Yes, for most stores. Shopify Analytics is strong on store-side and sales data; GA4 is stronger on full-funnel marketing attribution — which channels and campaigns actually drive the traffic that converts. They answer different questions and work best used together rather than as substitutes for each other.

What plan do I need for ShopifyQL and custom reports?

Full custom report building and the ShopifyQL editor are available on Shopify and Advanced-tier plans and above; entry-level plans get the standard canned reports. Check your current plan's reporting page to confirm what's included before assuming a feature is missing or paying for an app that duplicates it.

How often should I actually review analytics?

Daily sales checks are fine as a pulse check, but the KPIs that change your strategy — LTV, repeat rate, channel conversion — are better reviewed weekly or monthly. Daily noise in those numbers rarely means anything actionable, and over-reacting to daily swings often leads to strategy changes that aren't justified by the underlying trend.
